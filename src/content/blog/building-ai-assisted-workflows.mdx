## The Adoption Gap Nobody Talks About

Most AI feature projects I've seen follow the same arc: exciting demo, lukewarm adoption, eventual abandonment. The AI worked. The team just didn't use it. This is the adoption gap, and it's an engineering problem as much as a product problem.

The teams that succeed with AI-assisted workflows share one thing in common: they design for trust before they design for capability. An AI feature that is 90% accurate but unpredictable feels worse than one that is 85% accurate but consistent and transparent. Trust is built through track record, and track record requires time — which means you need a slow, deliberate rollout strategy, not a big launch.

## Start With One Narrow Workflow

The temptation is to automate broadly. Resist it. The right approach is to pick one workflow that is high-volume, repetitive, and has a clear success metric, then build the AI feature entirely around that workflow first.

For us, this was deal categorization. We were manually categorizing hundreds of merchant offers per week. The task was repetitive, the outputs were structured, and we had ground truth from historical human decisions to evaluate against. A narrow, well-scoped task with measurable quality — that's the sweet spot for AI-first automation.

## Keep a Human Approval Step While Confidence Builds

This sounds obvious, but teams consistently skip it to move faster. A human review gate on AI-generated outputs serves two purposes: it catches errors before they reach production, and it generates labeled data that you can use to evaluate and improve the model over time.

We ran our categorization agent in "suggest mode" for the first four weeks — it produced a label and confidence score, a human reviewer approved or overrode it. We tracked the override rate daily. When it dropped below 8% and stayed there for a week, we promoted the workflow to "auto-approve" for high-confidence outputs and kept human review only for low-confidence ones.

This graduated rollout approach meant the team saw the AI performing well before they trusted it with full autonomy. That visibility matters enormously for adoption.

## Define Output Quality Before You Build

One of the most common mistakes is building an AI feature without a clear definition of what "good output" means. You end up measuring the wrong things — task completion rate, API latency, token costs — and missing the quality signals that tell you whether the AI is actually helping.

Before writing a line of inference code, write a test suite that evaluates output quality against a held-out set of examples from your real data. For categorization, we had 200 human-labeled examples across 12 categories. We ran every prompt variant against this set before deploying anything. This gave us a baseline to compare against and a regression test that ran on every prompt change.

The broader principle: define what good looks like, measure it automatically, and monitor it in production with the same seriousness you'd monitor API uptime.

## Increase Automation Depth Gradually

Once quality is consistently measurable and consistently good, you can safely increase automation. But "increase automation" doesn't mean removing all human oversight — it means moving the human oversight to the edges rather than every individual output.

Our current state: the agent auto-processes high-confidence outputs, routes edge cases to a review queue, and sends a daily digest to the team with examples of AI decisions and any overrides from the previous day. The team stays informed without being in the loop on every single item.

The result: what previously took a team member several hours per week now takes under thirty minutes, with higher consistency than manual processing ever achieved. The AI didn't replace the human. It changed where the human's time goes.
